Introduction

Work, Energy and Power are three closely related physical quantities that describe how forces cause motion and how motion is sustained. This chapter builds the ideas from first principles: work done by constant and variable forces, the two forms of mechanical energy (kinetic and potential), the Work–Energy theorem, conservative and non-conservative forces, the law of conservation of mechanical energy, power, collisions, and motion in a vertical circle. Mastering these concepts and their derivations is essential for solving numerical problems of every difficulty level, since almost every problem in this chapter is built by combining two or three of these core ideas.

02

Work

2.1 Definition

Work is said to be done by a force when the force produces a displacement in the body on which it acts, or has a component along the displacement.

W = F · s = Fs cosθ

where F is the magnitude of the force, s is the magnitude of the displacement, and θ is the angle between the force vector and the displacement vector.

  • Work is a scalar quantity — it has magnitude but no direction (even though it is defined using the dot product of two vectors).
  • SI unit: joule (J) = N·m = kg m² s⁻². CGS unit: erg (1 J = 10⁷ erg).
  • Dimensional formula: [M L² T⁻²].

2.2 Sign of Work Done

Fig 2.1 — Positive, negative and zero work, depending on the angle between force and displacement.
  • Positive work (0° ≤ θ < 90°): the force has a component in the direction of motion. Example: gravity doing work on a freely falling body; an applied force pulling a cart forward.
  • Negative work (90° < θ ≤ 180°): the force opposes the motion. Example: friction acting on a sliding block; gravity acting on a ball thrown upward.
  • Zero work: occurs when (a) θ = 90°, force perpendicular to displacement — e.g. centripetal force in circular motion; (b) displacement is zero — e.g. pushing a wall that doesn't move; (c) the force itself is zero.

2.3 Work Done by a Constant Force

If a constant force F acts on a body that undergoes displacement s at a fixed angle θ to F, the work done is simply W = Fs cosθ. This is used directly whenever both force and angle stay the same throughout the motion.
