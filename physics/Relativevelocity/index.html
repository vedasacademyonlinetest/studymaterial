LEVEL 1 Same & Opposite Direction — Basics
Q 1
Two bikes A and B move in the same direction with speeds 20 m/s and 15 m/s. Find velocity of A relative to B.
🏍️ Same Direction | Relative Velocity
Given
  • v_A = 20 m/s (→)
  • v_B = 15 m/s (→)
  • Same direction
Find
  • v_A relative to B = ?
1
Key Rule: Relative velocity = velocity of A – velocity of B (when measuring how A appears to B).
2
v_AB = v_A − v_B = 20 − 15 = 5 m/s
3
Physical meaning: To a person sitting in bike B, bike A appears to move forward at only 5 m/s — like watching a slow race!
✅
v_A w.r.t B = 5 m/s (in same direction as motion)
🔑 GOLDEN RULE
Same direction → Subtract. Relative speed is always LESS than individual speeds.
Q 2
Two cars move in opposite directions with speeds 18 m/s and 12 m/s. Find their relative velocity.
🚗 Opposite Direction | Relative Velocity
Given
  • v_A = +18 m/s (→)
  • v_B = −12 m/s (←)
Find
  • v_A relative to B = ?
1
Assign signs: Right (→) is positive. Car B moving left = −12 m/s.
2
v_AB = v_A − v_B = 18 − (−12) = 18 + 12 = 30 m/s
3
Physical meaning: To a person in car B, car A seems to rush toward them at 30 m/s — much faster than either car's actual speed!
✅
Relative velocity = 30 m/s
🔑 GOLDEN RULE
Opposite directions → Add the speeds. Relative speed is always MORE than individual speeds.
LEVEL 2 Train + Person — Multi-Frame Problems
Q 3
Train moves at 20 m/s. A man runs on the train in the same direction at 5 m/s (relative to train). Find: (a) velocity of man w.r.t ground   (b) velocity of man w.r.t train
🚆 Frame Transfer | Train → Ground
Given
  • v_Train (ground) = 20 m/s
  • v_Man (w.r.t train) = 5 m/s
Find
  • v_Man (w.r.t ground)
  • v_Man (w.r.t train)
1
Frame Transfer Rule: v_Man(ground) = v_Train(ground) + v_Man(train)
2
v_Man(ground) = 20 + 5 = 25 m/s
3
Velocity w.r.t train is already given as 5 m/s (it's the input!).
v_Man(w.r.t train) = 5 m/s
✅
(a) v_Man w.r.t Ground = 25 m/s
(b) v_Man w.r.t Train = 5 m/s
Q 4
Same setup but man runs in the opposite direction at 5 m/s (relative to train).
🚆 Sign Change | Opposite Direction on Train
1
Man runs backward on train → v_Man(train) = −5 m/s (opposite to train motion)
2
v_Man(ground) = v_Train + v_Man(train) = 20 + (−5) = 15 m/s
3
Interpretation: Man is still moving forward relative to ground (15 m/s), but slower than the train.
✅
v_Man w.r.t Ground = 15 m/s (forward)
v_Man w.r.t Train = −5 m/s (backward on train)
⚠️ IMPORTANT
Direction on the train flips the sign! Always define positive direction clearly first.
LEVEL 3 Three Observers — Adding a Third Frame
Q 5
Train = 20 m/s, Man on train (forward) = 5 m/s, Car on ground (same direction) = 10 m/s. Find velocity of man relative to car.
🚆🚗 Three Frames | Man vs Car
1
First find man's velocity w.r.t ground:
v_Man(ground) = 20 + 5 = 25 m/s
2
Now find man's velocity relative to car:
v_Man(car) = v_Man(ground) − v_Car(ground) = 25 − 10 = 15 m/s
✅
v_Man relative to Car = 15 m/s (forward)
Q 6
Same data, but man runs backward (−5 m/s on train). Find velocity of man relative to car.
🚆🚗 Three Frames | Backward Man vs Car
1
Man's velocity w.r.t ground:
v_Man(ground) = 20 + (−5) = 15 m/s
2
Man's velocity relative to car:
v_Man(car) = 15 − 10 = 5 m/s
✅
v_Man relative to Car = 5 m/s (forward)
Q 7 🔥
Train = 20 m/s (→), Man = +5 m/s on train (→), Car = −10 m/s (←, opposite direction). Find velocity of man relative to car. ⚡ Concept Twist — 3 Layers
🔥 Multi-Layer Frame Analysis
1
Define positive as rightward (→).
2
Man's velocity w.r.t ground:
v_Man(ground) = 20 + 5 = 25 m/s
3
Car moves in opposite direction → v_Car = −10 m/s
4
Relative velocity of man w.r.t car:
v_Man(car) = v_Man(ground) − v_Car = 25 − (−10) = 25 + 10 = 35 m/s
✅
v_Man relative to Car = 35 m/s (→)
The car sees the man rushing at 35 m/s — like a rocket!
🧠 STRATEGY
Always reduce everything to ground frame first, then calculate relative velocity between any two objects.
LEVEL 4 River + Swimmer — Real Concept Problems
Q 8
River flows at 4 m/s. Swimmer swims at 6 m/s (relative to water) perpendicular to river. Find: (a) velocity seen by person on river bank   (b) direction of motion
🏊 2D Velocity | River Crossing
River ────────────────→ 4 m/s (downstream) ↑ ↑ 6 m/s (swimmer, perpendicular) ↑
1
Two velocities are perpendicular → use Pythagoras:
v_resultant = √(v_swim² + v_river²) = √(6² + 4²) = √(36 + 16) = √52 ≈ 7.2 m/s
2
Direction (angle with perpendicular bank):
tan θ = v_river / v_swim = 4 / 6 = 0.667 θ = tan⁻¹(0.667) ≈ 33.7° downstream
✅
(a) Speed seen by observer on bank ≈ 7.2 m/s
(b) Direction = 33.7° downstream from perpendicular
🔑 KEY IDEA
River carries the swimmer downstream even if he swims straight across. The actual path is the vector sum — always diagonal!
Q 9
River flows at 4 m/s. Swimmer swims upstream at 6 m/s. Find velocity w.r.t ground.
🏊 Upstream Motion
1
Swimmer and river are in opposite directions (1D problem).
v_swimmer(ground) = v_swimmer(water) − v_river = 6 − 4 = 2 m/s (upstream)
2
Interpretation: Ground observer sees the swimmer moving at only 2 m/s — river is "fighting" the swimmer.
✅
v_Swimmer w.r.t Ground = 2 m/s (upstream)
Q 10
Boat speed in still water = 10 m/s, River speed = 4 m/s. Find: (a) Upstream speed   (b) Downstream speed
🚤 Upstream & Downstream | Boat in River
1
Downstream (boat and river in same direction):
v_downstream = v_boat + v_river = 10 + 4 = 14 m/s
2
Upstream (boat fights against river):
v_upstream = v_boat − v_river = 10 − 4 = 6 m/s
✅
(a) Upstream speed = 6 m/s
(b) Downstream speed = 14 m/s
🔑 SHORTCUT
Downstream = boat + river  |  Upstream = boat − river
LEVEL 5 Mixed Frame Master Problems 🔥
Q 11
Train = 25 m/s (→). Man runs forward = 5 m/s. River flows perpendicular to track = 4 m/s (↑). Find velocity of man relative to stationary observer on river bank. ⚡ 2D Relative Velocity
🚆🌊 2D Vector Addition
↑ 4 m/s (river direction) | | ────────┼──────────────────→ Train direction (25+5 = 30 m/s) |
1
Man's velocity along track (horizontal):
v_x = v_Train + v_Man(on train) = 25 + 5 = 30 m/s
2
River flows perpendicular (vertical). The man is on the train — river doesn't move him! But the observer is ON the river bank, which is at rest. So the observer sees:
v_y component (observer has no extra velocity) = 0
The river flows but the observer stands still on the bank — they are NOT moving with the river.
3
The observer is stationary. Man has only horizontal velocity:
v_Man(w.r.t observer) = √(30² + 0²) = 30 m/s
✅
v_Man w.r.t stationary observer = 30 m/s (along track direction)
Note: River speed is irrelevant here — neither the man nor the observer is in the river.
Q 12
Boat speed w.r.t water = 8 m/s. River speed = 6 m/s. A man inside boat walks backward at 2 m/s (w.r.t boat). Find velocity of man w.r.t ground.
🚤 Three Frames | Boat-River-Man
1
Assume boat moves downstream (same direction as river).
v_Boat(ground) = v_Boat(water) + v_River = 8 + 6 = 14 m/s
2
Man walks backward on boat → v_Man(boat) = −2 m/s
v_Man(ground) = v_Boat(ground) + v_Man(boat) = 14 + (−2) = 12 m/s
✅
v_Man w.r.t Ground = 12 m/s (downstream)
📐 CHAIN RULE
v_Man(ground) = v_Man(boat) + v_Boat(water) + v_Water(ground)
Always build the chain from innermost frame to outermost (ground) frame.
Q 13 🔥
Train = 20 m/s (→). Man runs forward = 5 m/s. Car = 15 m/s (←). Wind = 10 m/s (→, direction of train). Find velocity of man relative to wind. ⚡ Olympiad Level — Multi-Frame + Tricky Reference
🌬️ Wind as Reference Frame
1
Man's velocity w.r.t ground:
v_Man(ground) = v_Train + v_Man(train) = 20 + 5 = 25 m/s (→)
2
Wind speed w.r.t ground = 10 m/s (→). Note: Car is a distractor here — not needed for this question!
3
Velocity of man relative to wind:
v_Man(wind) = v_Man(ground) − v_Wind(ground) = 25 − 10 = 15 m/s
✅
v_Man relative to Wind = 15 m/s (→)
⚠️ The car's velocity is a distractor — irrelevant to this specific question!
🎯 EXAM TIP
Olympiad questions include extra data to confuse you. Always identify EXACTLY which two objects you need, then calculate. Ignore the rest!
LEVEL 6 Visual Thinking — Advanced Conceptual
Q 14
Swimmer wants to cross river in shortest time. River speed = 3 m/s, Swimming speed = 5 m/s. Find: direction he should swim.
⏱️ Minimum Time Crossing
Bank ═══════════════════════════════ ↑ swimmer aims straight across (90°) | | ← river pushes 3 m/s → | Bank ═══════════════════════════════
1
Key insight: Time to cross = Width / (component of swimming velocity perpendicular to bank).
2
To minimize time → maximize the perpendicular component of swimming velocity.
3
The perpendicular component is maximum when the swimmer aims straight across (90° to bank) — regardless of river flow.
v_perpendicular = v_swim × cos(0°) = 5 m/s (maximum possible)
If width = d:   t_min = d / 5
4
He will land downstream (river carries him), but that's acceptable when the goal is shortest TIME, not shortest PATH.
✅
For minimum time → Swim STRAIGHT ACROSS (perpendicular to bank = 90°)
He will drift downstream but will cross fastest. River speed does not affect crossing time!
🔑 IMPORTANT DISTINCTION
Shortest TIME: Swim straight across (90° to bank)
Shortest PATH (minimum drift): Swim at an angle upstream to compensate for river current
Q 15
A boat aims directly at the opposite point but reaches downstream. Explain using relative velocity concept: (a) velocity seen by boatman   (b) velocity seen by ground observer
🚤 Conceptual | Frame-Dependent Velocity
Opposite Bank ──────────────[Target Point]────── ↑ (aimed here) ↑ (lands here, downstream) Boatman aims ──────╯ │ Actual path ╯ This Bank ────────────────[Start]──────────────────────────
1
(a) Velocity seen by boatman (his own reference):
The boatman is inside the boat. From his frame, he is pointing and moving directly toward the opposite bank — straight across. He does NOT feel the river pushing him. So in his frame, he appears to go straight.
v_Boat (w.r.t water) = directly perpendicular to bank
2
(b) Velocity seen by ground observer:
The ground observer sees BOTH the boat's perpendicular velocity AND the river's horizontal flow. These add as vectors.
v_Boat(ground) = v_Boat(water) + v_River(ground)
This vector sum is diagonal — pointing downstream of the target.
3
Why does boat land downstream? Because the river continuously pushes the boat sideways. The ground observer sees this drift; the boatman inside the boat does NOT feel it — he just sees himself going straight.
✅
(a) Boatman sees himself going straight across (perpendicular to bank) — river current is invisible to him.

(b) Ground observer sees diagonal motion = vector sum of boat + river. This diagonal path takes the boat downstream of target.
💡 BIG CONCEPT
Velocity is frame-dependent. The same motion looks completely different from different reference frames. This is the heart of relative velocity!
