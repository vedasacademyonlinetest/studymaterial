Q1 Equal Diagonals · Rhombus vs Square
ASSERTION (A)
A rhombus whose diagonals are equal in length is always a square.
REASON (R)
In a rhombus, the diagonals are perpendicular bisectors of each other. If the diagonals are also equal, all four vertex angles each become 90°, making the figure a square.
(a) Both A and R are true, and R is the correct explanation of A
(b) Both A and R are true, but R is NOT the correct explanation of A
(c) A is true, R is false
(d) A is false, R is true
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(a) Both true; R correctly explains A
Explanation

In a rhombus, let the diagonals be d₁ and d₂. Since diagonals are perpendicular bisectors of each other, they divide the rhombus into four congruent right triangles, each with legs d₁/2 and d₂/2.


Each vertex angle of the rhombus equals 2 × arctan(d₁/d₂) or 2 × arctan(d₂/d₁) depending on which vertex. If d₁ = d₂, then arctan(d₁/d₂) = arctan(1) = 45°, so every vertex angle = 90°. A rhombus (all sides equal) with all angles 90° is exactly a square.

Core Concept
Rhombus + Equal Diagonals ⟹ Square. The diagonals being perpendicular bisectors is the structural reason why equal diagonals force all angles to 90°.
Memory Trick
Rhombus Upgrade Rule: Add "perpendicular diagonals" → still rhombus. Add "equal diagonals" → becomes square. Both properties live in a square!
Q2 Rectangle Diagonal Misconception
ASSERTION (A)
The diagonals of a rectangle are perpendicular to each other.
REASON (R)
The diagonals of a rectangle are equal in length and bisect each other.
(a) Both A and R are true, and R is the correct explanation of A
(b) Both A and R are true, but R is NOT the correct explanation of A
(c) A is true, R is false
(d) A is false, R is true
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(d) A is false; R is true
Why A is FALSE

Rectangle diagonals are equal and bisect each other, but they are NOT perpendicular — unless the rectangle is also a square. For a 3×4 rectangle: diagonal slope = 4/3; other diagonal slope = −4/3. Product = −16/9 ≠ −1. Not perpendicular!

Why R is TRUE

R is a correct and well-known property. Both diagonals of a rectangle have the same length (provable by Pythagoras), and as a parallelogram, its diagonals bisect each other.

Common Misconception
Students confuse "perpendicular diagonals" (property of rhombus, kite) with "equal diagonals" (property of rectangle). These are different properties that only coexist in a square.
Core Concept
Rectangle: Equal diagonals + bisect each other (NOT perpendicular). Rhombus: Perpendicular diagonals + bisect each other (NOT equal). Square: ALL four diagonal properties.
Q3 Acute Triangle · Side² Relation vs Altitude Property
ASSERTION (A)
For a triangle with sides a, b, c (where c is the longest side), if a² + b² > c², the triangle is acute-angled.
REASON (R)
In an acute triangle, all three altitudes lie entirely inside the triangle.
(a) Both A and R are true, and R is the correct explanation of A
(b) Both A and R are true, but R is NOT the correct explanation of A
(c) A is true, R is false
(d) A is false, R is true
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(b) Both true; R does NOT explain A
Why A is TRUE

By the generalized Law of Cosines: cos C = (a² + b² − c²) / (2ab). If a² + b² > c², then cos C > 0, so ∠C < 90°. Since C is the largest angle (opposite longest side c), all other angles are also less than 90°. Hence the triangle is acute.

Why R is TRUE but NOT the explanation

R states a consequence of being acute — it describes what acute triangles look like geometrically. However, R does not tell us why the algebraic condition a² + b² > c² leads to an acute triangle. The explanation for A lies in trigonometry (Law of Cosines), not in the altitude property.

Core Concept

Acutea² + b² > c²
Righta² + b² = c²
Obtusea² + b² < c²
Q4 Bisector vs Perpendicular Bisector · Circumcenter
ASSERTION (A)
The perpendicular bisector of side BC of any triangle ABC always passes through vertex A.
REASON (R)
The circumcenter of a triangle is equidistant from all three vertices, and it is the point of concurrence of the perpendicular bisectors of the three sides.
(a) Both A and R are true, and R is the correct explanation of A
(b) Both A and R are true, but R is NOT the correct explanation of A
(c) A is true, R is false
(d) A is false, R is true
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(d) A is false; R is true
Why A is FALSE

The perpendicular bisector of BC passes through the circumcenter, not through vertex A. It passes through A only in the special case when AB = AC (isosceles triangle), because then A is equidistant from B and C, placing it on the perpendicular bisector of BC.


For a scalene triangle (AB ≠ AC), the perpendicular bisector of BC passes nowhere near A.

Why R is TRUE

R is a standard and correct theorem. The circumcenter O satisfies OA = OB = OC (circumradius), and the three perpendicular bisectors meet exactly at O.

Classic Trap
Students confuse "perpendicular bisector of BC" with "angle bisector from A" or "altitude from A." None of these are the same line in a general triangle.
Key Distinction
Altitude from A → perpendicular to BC, but from vertex A (not mid-BC).
Perpendicular bisector of BC → passes through mid-BC, perpendicular to BC, but NOT through A (in general).
Angle bisector from A → bisects ∠A, passes through A, but is not perpendicular to BC.
Q5 Kite · Perpendicular Bisector of Diagonal
ASSERTION (A)
In kite ABCD with AB = AD and CB = CD, the diagonal AC is the perpendicular bisector of diagonal BD.
REASON (R)
In a kite, the "axis diagonal" (the one connecting the vertices where unequal sides meet) bisects the other diagonal at right angles, by the symmetry of the figure.
(a) Both A and R are true, and R is the correct explanation of A
(b) Both A and R are true, but R is NOT the correct explanation of A
(c) A is true, R is false
(d) A is false, R is true
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(a) Both true; R correctly explains A
Explanation

Since AB = AD, vertex A lies on the perpendicular bisector of BD. Since CB = CD, vertex C also lies on the perpendicular bisector of BD. Two distinct points (A and C) both lying on the perpendicular bisector of BD determine that line — which is exactly the line AC.


Therefore, AC is the perpendicular bisector of BD. This means: (1) AC ⊥ BD, and (2) AC bisects BD at their intersection point. The kite's line of symmetry (AC) reflects B onto D.

Important Distinction
In a kite, AC is the perpendicular bisector of BD, but BD is NOT the perpendicular bisector of AC. BD bisects AC only in a rhombus or square. In a kite, BD merely intersects AC perpendicularly (without bisecting it).
Kite Diagonal Summary
Diagonal AC (axis): bisects ∠A and ∠C; is perpendicular bisector of BD.
Diagonal BD: bisected by AC; perpendicular to AC; does NOT bisect AC.

Q8 Coordinate Geometry · Parallelogram Verification
⚑ One or more options are correct.
Quadrilateral ABCD has vertices A(0,0), B(4,0), C(5,3), D(1,3). Which of the following statements are TRUE?
(A) ABCD is a parallelogram
(B) ABCD is a rectangle
(C) The diagonals of ABCD bisect each other
(D) The diagonals of ABCD are equal in length
(E) The diagonals of ABCD are perpendicular
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(A) and (C)
Step-by-step Verification
AB: A(0,0)→B(4,0) · slope = 0 · length = 4 CD: C(5,3)→D(1,3) · slope = 0 · length = 4 → AB ∥ CD, |AB| = |CD| ✓ BC: B(4,0)→C(5,3) · slope = 3 · length = √10 DA: D(1,3)→A(0,0) · slope = 3 · length = √10 → BC ∥ DA, |BC| = |DA| ✓

Both pairs of opposite sides are parallel and equal → ABCD is a parallelogram ✓ (A correct)


Midpoint of AC: ((0+5)/2, (0+3)/2) = (2.5, 1.5) Midpoint of BD: ((4+1)/2, (0+3)/2) = (2.5, 1.5) → Diagonals bisect each other ✓ (C correct)

B is false: Slope of AB = 0, slope of BC = 3. Product = 0, not −1. Sides are not perpendicular → not a rectangle.


AC = √((5−0)² + (3−0)²) = √(25+9) = √34 BD = √((1−4)² + (3−0)²) = √(9+9) = √18 = 3√2 √34 ≠ 3√2 → Diagonals NOT equal ✗ (D false)

E is false: Slope of AC = 3/5, slope of BD = −1. Product = −3/5 ≠ −1. Not perpendicular.

Key Takeaway
A parallelogram always has bisecting diagonals (C follows from A), but it need not have equal or perpendicular diagonals — those are special properties of rectangles and rhombuses respectively.
Q9 Obtuse Triangle · Side Square Conditions
⚑ One or more options are correct.
A triangle has sides a ≤ b ≤ c. Which of the following conditions individually guarantee the triangle is obtuse?
(A) a² + b² < c²
(B) a² + b² = c²
(C) c² − b² > a²
(D) b² > a² + c²
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(A) and (C)
Analysis of Each Option

(A) a² + b² < c² — Rearranges to c² > a² + b², i.e., the cosine of angle C is negative → ∠C > 90°. ✓ Obtuse


(B) a² + b² = c² — Pythagoras → ∠C = 90°. This is a right triangle, not obtuse. ✗


(C) c² − b² > a² — This rearranges to c² > a² + b², which is identical to option (A). ✓ Obtuse


(D) b² > a² + c² — Since b ≤ c, we have b² ≤ c². So b² > a² + c² would require c² < b² − a² ≤ b² ≤ c², a contradiction. This condition is impossible for a valid triangle with a ≤ b ≤ c. ✗ Impossible

Core Concept
In a triangle with a ≤ b ≤ c, the only angle that can be obtuse is ∠C (opposite the longest side). Checking a² + b² vs c² is the definitive test.
Q10 Right Triangle · Multiple Properties
⚑ One or more options are correct.
In a right triangle with legs 3 and 4, which of the following statements are TRUE?
(A) The hypotenuse has length 5
(B) The median drawn to the hypotenuse has length 2.5
(C) The circumradius of the triangle equals 5
(D) The altitude drawn to the hypotenuse has length 2.4
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(A), (B), and (D)
Verification

(A) Hypotenuse = √(3² + 4²) = √25 = 5 ✓


(B) Key theorem: In a right triangle, the median to the hypotenuse equals half the hypotenuse. Median = 5/2 = 2.5 ✓


(C) False! The circumradius of a right triangle = hypotenuse/2 = 5/2 = 2.5, NOT 5. (The hypotenuse is the diameter of the circumscribed circle.) ✗


(D) Area = (1/2)(3)(4) = 6. Also, Area = (1/2) × hypotenuse × altitude to hypotenuse = (1/2)(5)(h). So h = 12/5 = 2.4 ✓

Option C Trap
Circumradius = hypotenuse/2 (for right triangles). Many students confuse the hypotenuse (5) with the circumradius (2.5). The hypotenuse is the diameter, not the radius.
Golden Formula Trio for 3-4-5 triangle
Median to hypotenuse = Circumradius = 2.5  |  Altitude to hypotenuse = 2.4  |  Inradius = (3+4−5)/2 = 1

Q11 Medial Triangle · Right Triangle Inheritance
The medial triangle of a right-angled triangle (the triangle formed by joining the midpoints of its three sides) is always:
(a) Acute-angled
(b) Right-angled
(c) Obtuse-angled
(d) Equilateral
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(b) Right-angled
Proof

Let the original right triangle have legs a, b and hypotenuse c, where a² + b² = c². By the Midpoint Theorem, the medial triangle has sides a/2, b/2, c/2.


Test: (a/2)² + (b/2)² = a²/4 + b²/4 = (a²+b²)/4 = c²/4 = (c/2)²


The sides satisfy Pythagoras, so the medial triangle is also right-angled. In fact, it is similar to the original triangle with ratio 1:2, so it inherits the same angle measures.

Generalisation
The medial triangle is always similar to the original triangle (with a ratio of 1:2). So whatever the type of the original — acute, right, or obtuse — the medial triangle is the same type.
Q12 Coordinate Geometry · Square Identification
The vertices of quadrilateral PQRS are P(1,0), Q(0,1), R(−1,0), S(0,−1). What is the most specific classification of PQRS?
(a) Rhombus (but not square)
(b) Rectangle (but not square)
(c) Square
(d) Kite
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(c) Square
Full Verification
PQ = √((1−0)²+(0−1)²) = √2 QR = √((0+1)²+(1−0)²) = √2 RS = √((−1−0)²+(0+1)²) = √2 SP = √((0−1)²+(−1−0)²) = √2 → All sides equal → Rhombus family Diagonal PR: P(1,0)→R(−1,0), length = 2, along x-axis, slope = 0 Diagonal QS: Q(0,1)→S(0,−1), length = 2, along y-axis, slope = undefined (vertical) Both diagonals have length 2 → Equal diagonals PR ⊥ QS (x-axis ⊥ y-axis) → Perpendicular diagonals

All sides equal + equal diagonals + perpendicular diagonals → Square. Also, midpoint of PR = (0,0) = midpoint of QS → diagonals bisect each other.

Square Checklist (any one pair confirms after sides are equal)
✓ All sides equal → Rhombus
✓ Equal diagonals → upgrades to Square
✓ Perpendicular diagonals → already had (rhombus property)
✓ All angles 90° → confirmed Square
Q13 Parallelogram · Perpendicular Bisector of Diagonal
In parallelogram ABCD, diagonals AC and BD intersect at O. The perpendicular bisector of AC:
(a) Always passes through vertices B and D
(b) Is always parallel to BD
(c) Always passes through the midpoint of BD
(d) Is always perpendicular to BD
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(c) Always passes through the midpoint of BD
Reasoning

Since ABCD is a parallelogram, the diagonals bisect each other. So O is both the midpoint of AC and the midpoint of BD.


The perpendicular bisector of AC, by definition, passes through the midpoint of AC, which is O. Since O is also the midpoint of BD, the perpendicular bisector of AC always passes through the midpoint of BD.

Why (a) is wrong
The perp. bisector of AC passes through B and D only if OB = OA = OC, i.e., when AC = BD (only in a rectangle or square, not all parallelograms).
Why (b) and (d) are wrong
The perpendicular bisector of AC is perpendicular to AC. Whether it is parallel or perpendicular to BD depends on the specific parallelogram — there is no guarantee in general.
Q14 Area Formula · Perpendicular Diagonals (Any Quadrilateral)
A quadrilateral has diagonals of lengths p and q that intersect at right angles (but not necessarily at each other's midpoints). Its area is:
(a) pq
(b) ½ pq
(c) ¼ pq
(d) Cannot be determined without the intersection point
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(b) ½ pq
Elegant Proof

Let diagonals intersect at X, dividing one diagonal into segments a + c = p and the other into b + d = q, with a right angle at X.


The quadrilateral is split into 4 right triangles with areas:

Area₁ = ½ab, Area₂ = ½bc, Area₃ = ½cd, Area₄ = ½da Total = ½(ab + bc + cd + da) = ½ · b(a+c) + ½ · d(a+c) = ½(a+c)(b+d) = ½ · p · q

This result is independent of where exactly on the diagonals they intersect! The position of X cancels out algebraically.

Power Result
Area of any quadrilateral with perpendicular diagonals d₁ and d₂ = ½d₁d₂, regardless of intersection point. This is why rhombus, kite, and square areas all use this formula.
Q15 Triangle Inequality · Degenerate Triangle Trap
Which of the following sets of lengths CANNOT form the sides of a triangle?
(a) 3, 4, 6
(b) 5, 8, 12
(c) 7, 8, 9
(d) 6, 7, 13
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(d) 6, 7, 13
Triangle Inequality Check

A valid triangle requires: sum of any two sides strictly greater than the third side.

(a) 3+4=7 > 6 ✓, 3+6=9 > 4 ✓, 4+6=10 > 3 ✓ → Valid triangle (b) 5+8=13 > 12 ✓, 5+12=17 > 8 ✓, 8+12=20 > 5 ✓ → Valid triangle (c) 7+8=15 > 9 ✓, 7+9=16 > 8 ✓, 8+9=17 > 7 ✓ → Valid triangle (d) 6+7=13 = 13, NOT strictly greater → DEGENERATE → NOT a valid triangle ✗
The Degenerate Case
When the sum of two sides equals the third (6+7=13), the triangle collapses into a straight line — a degenerate case. The condition requires strict inequality (>, not ≥).
Note on (b)
Option (b) is a valid triangle (5+8=13 > 12 — barely!). Students often suspect (b) as the trap, but 13 > 12 is strictly satisfied.
Q16 Quadrilateral Identification · Equal Opposite Sides + Equal Diagonals
In quadrilateral ABCD, AB = CD, BC = AD, and the diagonals satisfy AC = BD. Which of the following must be true?
(a) ABCD is a parallelogram only
(b) ABCD is a rectangle
(c) ABCD is a rhombus
(d) ABCD is a square
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(b) Rectangle
Step-by-step reasoning

Step 1: AB = CD and BC = AD (both pairs of opposite sides equal) → ABCD is a parallelogram.


Step 2: In a parallelogram, if the diagonals are equal (AC = BD), then the parallelogram is a rectangle.


Proof of Step 2: In parallelogram ABCD, triangles ABC and DCB share side BC. AB = DC (opposite sides), BC = BC (common). If also AC = BD, then △ABC ≅ △DCB (SSS). So ∠ABC = ∠DCB. But co-interior angles AB∥DC give ∠ABC + ∠DCB = 180°, so ∠ABC = ∠DCB = 90°. All angles are 90° → Rectangle.

Why not rhombus or square?
We have no information that all four sides are equal, so we can't conclude rhombus or square. A rectangle that isn't a square is a valid answer (e.g., 3×5 rectangle).
Upgrade Chain
Equal opposite sides → Parallelogram
Parallelogram + equal diagonals → Rectangle
Rectangle + all sides equal → Square
Q17 Midpoint Formula · Varignon's Parallelogram
The vertices of a square are A(0,0), B(2a,0), C(2a,2a), D(0,2a) where a > 0. The quadrilateral formed by joining the midpoints of its four sides (in order) is:
(a) A rectangle but not a square
(b) A rhombus but not a square
(c) A square
(d) A parallelogram but not a rectangle
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(c) A square
Calculation
Midpoint of AB = M₁ = (a, 0) Midpoint of BC = M₂ = (2a, a) Midpoint of CD = M₃ = (a, 2a) Midpoint of DA = M₄ = (0, a) M₁M₂ = √(a²+a²) = a√2, slope = a/a = 1 M₂M₃ = √(a²+a²) = a√2, slope = a/(−a) = −1 M₃M₄ = √(a²+a²) = a√2, slope = a/a = 1 M₄M₁ = √(a²+a²) = a√2, slope = a/(−a) = −1

All four sides equal (a√2). Adjacent sides have slopes 1 and −1 → product = −1 → perpendicular. All angles = 90°, all sides equal → Square.

Varignon's Theorem (Extended)
The midpoints of any quadrilateral form a parallelogram (Varignon's Theorem). If the original quadrilateral's diagonals are perpendicular, the midpoint parallelogram is a rectangle. If the diagonals are equal, it is a rhombus. If both — it is a square. Since a square has perpendicular AND equal diagonals, its midpoint figure is a square.
Q18 Isosceles Trapezium · Identification
In quadrilateral ABCD: AB ∥ CD, AB ≠ CD, and AC = BD. Which of the following necessarily follows?
(a) ABCD is a rectangle
(b) ABCD is a parallelogram
(c) ABCD is an isosceles trapezium
(d) The diagonals of ABCD bisect each other
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(c) Isosceles trapezium
Analysis

AB ∥ CD and AB ≠ CD → exactly one pair of parallel sides → this is a trapezium (not a parallelogram).


The distinguishing property of an isosceles trapezium among all trapezoids is that the diagonals are equal: AC = BD. Combined with AB ∥ CD, ABCD is an isosceles trapezium.

Why (b) and (d) fail
Since AB ≠ CD, ABCD cannot be a parallelogram (parallelogram requires AB = CD when AB ∥ CD). And since it's not a parallelogram, its diagonals do not bisect each other.
Isosceles Trapezium Signature Properties
① Exactly one pair of parallel sides (AB ∥ CD)
② Non-parallel sides (legs) are equal: AD = BC
③ Equal diagonals: AC = BD
④ Base angles are equal: ∠DAB = ∠CBA
⑤ Symmetric about the perpendicular bisector of AB and CD
Q19 Distance Formula · Symmetric Points
The distance between the points A(a, b) and B(b, a) is:
(a) |a − b|
(b) √2 · |a − b|
(c) 2|a − b|
(d) (a − b)²
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(b) √2 · |a − b|
Calculation
AB = √((a−b)² + (b−a)²) = √((a−b)² + (a−b)²) [since (b−a)² = (a−b)²] = √(2(a−b)²) = |a−b| · √2
Geometric Insight

Points A(a,b) and B(b,a) are reflections of each other across the line y = x. The line y = x has slope 1, and AB is perpendicular to it (slope −1). The distance from any point (a,b) to the line y = x is |a−b|/√2, and the total distance AB = twice this = √2 · |a−b|.

Quick Check with Numbers
Try a=3, b=1: A(3,1), B(1,3). AB = √(4+4) = √8 = 2√2. Formula: √2·|3−1| = 2√2. ✓
Q20 Surd Sides · Hidden Pythagorean Triple
A triangle has sides of length √3, 2, and √7. Identify the type of this triangle.
(a) Acute-angled
(b) Right-angled
(c) Obtuse-angled
(d) The triangle is invalid (cannot exist)
▶ Show Solution
ANSWER
(b) Right-angled
Verification
Sides: √3 ≈ 1.732, 2, √7 ≈ 2.646 Longest side: √7 Test Pythagoras: (√3)² + 2² = 3 + 4 = 7 = (√7)² ✓

The sides exactly satisfy a² + b² = c² → the triangle is right-angled, with the right angle between sides √3 and 2.

Validity Check
Triangle inequality: √3 + 2 ≈ 3.73 > √7 ≈ 2.65 ✓ √3 + √7 ≈ 4.38 > 2 ✓ 2 + √7 ≈ 4.65 > √3 ✓ → Valid triangle
Why (a) and (c) are wrong
Students who approximate (√3 ≈ 1.7, √7 ≈ 2.6) may not recognize the exact sum 3 + 4 = 7. Always compute the exact squares of surd sides — never approximate!
